User: What's the difference between TCP and UDP protocols?

User: How does public key cryptography work?
Assistant: Public key cryptography, also called asymmetric cryptography, solves the key distribution problem that plagued symmetric cryptography for millennia. The fundamental insight is using mathematically related but distinct keys: a public key that can be freely shared and a private key that must remain secret. Anything encrypted with the public key can only be decrypted with the corresponding private key, and vice versa for digital signatures. The security relies on mathematical problems believed to be computationally infeasible to reverse without the private key. RSA, invented by Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman in 1977, bases its security on the practical difficulty of factoring the product of two large prime numbers. Key generation involves selecting two large primes p and q, computing n = p×q as the modulus, and φ(n) = (p-1)(q-1). Choose public exponent e coprime to φ(n), typically 65537. The private exponent d is the modular multiplicative inverse of e modulo φ(n). Encryption of message m is c = mᵉ mod n, and decryption is m = cᵈ mod n. The security of 2048-bit RSA is believed equivalent to approximately 112 bits of symmetric security. Elliptic Curve Cryptography offers equivalent security with much smaller key sizes by using the algebraic structure of elliptic curves over finite fields. The discrete logarithm problem on elliptic curves is harder than integer factorization per bit. Diffie-Hellman key exchange enables two parties to establish a shared secret over an insecure channel without prior arrangement, using the difficulty of discrete logarithms. In practice, hybrid cryptosystems combine asymmetric and symmetric techniques: asymmetric cryptography establishes a session key, then symmetric algorithms like AES-GCM handle bulk data encryption efficiently. Digital signatures provide authentication, integrity, and non-repudiation. Quantum computing poses a threat to current public key systems through Shor's algorithm, driving development of post-quantum cryptography based on lattice problems, code-based cryptography, and multivariate equations. User: What is the Central Limit Theorem and why is it important?
Assistant: The Central Limit Theorem is arguably the most important result in statistical inference and one of the most remarkable theorems in all of mathematics. It states that the sampling distribution of the sample mean approaches a normal distribution as the sample size increases, regardless of the shape of the original population distribution, provided the samples are independent and identically distributed with finite variance. More formally, if X₁, X₂, ..., Xₙ are i.i.d. random variables with mean μ and variance σ², then as n → ∞, the distribution of (X̄ - μ) / (σ/√n) converges to the standard normal distribution N(0,1). The convergence rate depends on the underlying distribution's skewness and kurtosis, but for sample sizes n ≥ 30, the approximation is often sufficient for practical purposes. This theorem justifies using normal distribution-based confidence intervals and hypothesis tests even when the population is not normally distributed. It explains why measurement errors tend to be normally distributed as the sum of many small independent factors. It underlies quality control charts in manufacturing, election polling with margins of error, and the ubiquitous use of z-scores and t-tests in scientific research. There are important caveats: the theorem requires finite variance, so it doesn't apply to heavy-tailed distributions like Cauchy. The Lyapunov and Lindeberg conditions extend the theorem to non-identically distributed variables with certain regularity conditions. The multivariate Central Limit Theorem extends it to random vectors. In the age of big data, the theorem remains foundational to the bootstrap method, Bayesian posterior approximation, and asymptotic properties of maximum likelihood estimators.
